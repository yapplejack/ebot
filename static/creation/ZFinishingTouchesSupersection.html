Finishing and Tuning the Arm

Before the Everybot’s arm is fully operational, the following things need to be added:

  • Add U-bolts
  • Add steel cable and constrain tension to 23”
  • Add the latex surgical tubing and tighten to ~22” total
  • Find hardstop locations and string rope
  • Install and attach badge clips
  • Tune all turnbuckles and hard stops
  • Tighten surgical tubing to prevent arm backdrive

Three additional U-bolts will be added to the superstructure that will be used to string the length of rope used to hard stop the arm in both the stowed and extended position. The first two U-bolts go on the 02_Superstructure_Support with the lower hole being centered three inches from the uncut top of 02_Superstructure_Support. Depending on the U-bolts used the location of the upper hole will change, so make sure to measure the U-bolt to determine the location of the 2nd hole. The U should point towards the back of the robot. When attaching the U-bolt use two additional 1/4”-20 nuts on top of the included hardware.

The final U-Bolt is centered vertically and horizontally on the 02_Superstrucutre_Top_Crossbeam with the U facing towards the back of the robot.

The steel cable runs through the 03_Arm_Tension_Unit and is one of two pieces that can be tuned to achieve the proper arm positions. The steel cable works by controlling the length of 03_Arm_Tension_Unit.

The steel cable is run behind the Piano Hinges when viewing the robot from the front. To get the correct length of steel cable, run a full loop through the four holes in 03_Arm_Tension_Unit and leave about 8” extra. Cut the steel cable with a pair of sideways cutters or other sharp cutting tool.

Open one of the turnbuckles evenly to halfway on both sides. Feed the steel cable through both sides such that the turnbuckle is centered in the middle of the 03_Arm_Tension_Unit and there is equal cable looped through both sides of the turnbuckle. The steel cable should be pulled such that there is no slack but it does not compress the 03_Arm_Tension_Unit. When in the proper position slide two aluminum ferrules down one side of the wire such that it looks like the leftmost picture below. The ferrules can be crimped using clamping pliers, needle nose pliers or anything else that exerts enough force to close the ferrule. Once both sides have crimped, you should be able to tighten the turnbuckle to compress the 03_Arm_Tension_Unit. Compress the 03_Arm_Tension_Unit until there is about 23” between the centers of the piano hinges.

Once the steel cable is in place, the Latex Hose surgical tubing is added to help spring the arm towards its stowed and extended positions. The end goal is for the Latex Hose to be tight enough to counteract gravity and prevent the arm from moving in either position while still allowing the arm motor to swing the arm. For now, tightly wrap the Latex Hose around the 03_Arm_Closeout and 02_Superstrucutre_Top_Crossbeam and either tie it into a knot or use zip ties to secure it. The hose will be tuned further after the hard stops are in place.

 

Before installing the hard stops, let’s explain a little bit further what the hard stop looks like and what it accomplishes.

  • What does the hardstop look like?

The hardstop consists of two lengths of Dyneema cable (billed as rope) with one end of each rope tied to one of the two U-bolts added to the 02_Superstructure_Supports. The ropes run through the U-bolts in the  03_Arm_Driver_Links and then meet in the middle of the robot where both ropes are tied to opposite ends of a turnbuckle. The turnbuckle is used to adjust the overall length of the ropes and two retracting I.D. badge clips keep the rope and turnbuckle out of the rest of the mechanism while swinging.

  • What does the hardstop do?

The hardstop limits the movement of the arm both forwards and backwards. When the arm is driven into the robot the hardstop prevents the intake from crashing into the 02_Superstructure_Mid_Crossbeam. When driven out of the robot the hardstop both prevents the robot from damaging the arm and positions the intake to pick up from the shelf or score on the high row. The act of finding the correct position for the intake is called “tuning” and will consist of adjusting both the steel cable turnbuckle and the rope hardstop turnbuckle. The primary goal of tuning is to ensure that as the robot approaches an upright cone on the loading zone platform, the front roller contacts the very tip of the cone first and sucks it into the second roller, pulling the cone up and into the mechanism.

Below is a diagram of the end goal. The distance between the center pins of the piano hinges should be 23” with the center of the outermost 04_Intake_Roller_Shaft 50.75” inches off the ground and 39.75” from the end of the chassis.

There are many potential ways to tune the Everybot, but here is a simple method that uses a speed square and a tape measure. Clamp a leftover piece of tubestock or other long straight object perpendicular to a table or up against a wall. Make sure the vertical object is square using a speed square and then use your tape measure to mark a line 50.75” up (in these diagrams, the upright box tube is simply 50.75” tall). Use a tape measure to line the front of the chassis up 39.75 inches from the bottom edge of the vertical object.

Once the robot is in position, have one person push the arm out until the center of the outermost roller is in line with the front face of the vertical object. This is the easiest position to install the rope and turnbuckle that make up the arm’s hard stop, and the latex tubing added earlier should hold the arm in place. Don’t worry if the center of the roller does not line up with the mark or cutoff (like shown), this height will be adjusted using the turnbuckle on the 03_Arm_Tension_Unit after the Dyneema cable and turnbuckles are added.


When attaching the Dyneema cable, use a half fishman’s knot on both the U-bolts and the turnbuckle. Below are the different stages of the knot. In the third step, 5-6 loops are recommended. After step three, slide the knot towards the U-bolt and pull tight. Leave extra rope on both sides until the final hardstop position is found. The ends of the rope can be singed with a lighter to prevent fraying.

        

Secure the rope to both sides of the superstructure using the U-bolts on the 02_Superstructure_Supports. Open the second turnbuckle about halfway and make sure the arm is in the position mentioned previously. Feed the ropes through the U-bolts on the 03_Arm_Driver_Links and use half fisherman’s knots on both sides of the turnbuckle. The rope should be taught on both sides and the turnbuckle should be roughly centered in the robot.

Once the turnbuckle has been connected on both sides, test the stowing position of the hardstop. The arm should be locked out approximately in this position to about 15 degrees past parallel with the chassis, the intake should not hit the 02_Superstructure_Mid_Crossbeam, and the intake should not extend over the vertical plane created by the chassis. If the intake crashes, see the arm adjustment section below.

To prevent the rope and turnbuckles from getting tangled in the rest of the robot while the arm swings back and forth, clip the two I.D. badge clips to the U-bolt on the 02_Superstructure_Top_Crossbeam and then clip the retracting string to the rope near where they attach to the turnbuckles.

Arm Adjustments

If the intake is close to the proper height when fully extended and hitting the hard stop but the stowed position hardstop is too far away to prevent the intake from hitting the 02_Superstructure_Mid_Crossbeam, try flipping the direction of the U-bolts on the 02_Superstructure_Supports. Untie the rope from the hardstop turnbuckle, make sure the turnbuckle is about half open, and find the new position to retie it when the arm is in the collecting position using the method described previously.

If this does not fix the stowing issue the U-bolts can also be moved on the 02_Superstructure_Support. This can be done if the height of the intake is fairly close to the desired location when the arm is in the collecting position. If the collecting and scoring height is off it will be best to wait until later to reposition the U-bolts.

Now that we have all adjustment mechanisms in place we will double check that we have the valid starting lengths and then adjust for the ideal geometry.

First, double check to make sure that the center to center Piano Hinge distance on the secondary linkage of the 4 bar mechanism is as close to 23” as you can. This will allow the arm to swing along the desired arc.

Now focus on using the adjustable turnbuckle on the arm hardstop to limit how far out the arm is able to move. The front roller should be at 50.75” off the ground, and 39.75” away from the front of the drivebase.

The primary goal in these adjustments is to ensure that as the robot approaches an upright cone on the loading zone platform, the outermost roller contacts the very tip of the cone first and sucks it into the middle roller, pulling the cone up and into the mechanism.

Let's take a look at how adjusting these linkages impacts the position and movement of the front roller.

Shortening the 03_Arm_Tension_Unit using the turnbuckle on the steel cable, shown via the first orange arrow, will cause the first roller to move further up and in due to the long lever arm that the 03_Arm_Main_Link and the intake itself form. Conversely, lengthening this linkage will result in the first roller dropping and swinging in a wider arc. In both cases, the roller will move along a circular path like shown in purple above as the intake pivots about the rear linkage pin.

Shortening the primary hardstop will cause the roller to stop sooner in its movement. As pictured, shortening the primary hardstop will cause the front roller to move down and backwards slightly. It is our suggestion to use this as your primary tool to change the position of the intake rollers, as changing the length of the linkage changes the shape of the whole mechanism's movement. It is significantly more important to hit the correct roller height if you are having trouble hitting both.

If done correctly, the robot should be able to pick from the loading zone, and score in the high levels, for both cubes and cones, with the arm fully outstretched. If the distance from the robot is off you may have trouble scoring cones. If you find that the cone you are trying to score is hitting the cone node in the middle row before reaching the high row, the arm should be tuned for further outwards reach by loosening the hard stop turnbuckle or otherwise lengthening the reach of the arm. Don’t forget to check the stowing position as the robot is required to start inside its frame at the beginning of the match. The U-bolts on 02_Superstructure_Supports may need to be flipped or moved if the correct arm geometry has been achieved but the robot does not stow.

4 Bar Linkage Springs

Once the arm has been turned, the latex surgical tubing will be retensioned to assist in keeping the arm in position better. The Everybot uses surgical tubing or other springs to make the arm capable of holding itself both fully extended and fully retracted. It does this by taking advantage of a quirk of geometry that creates two locations where the same spring pulls the mechanism towards the each end of travel.

In the above image, the springs (represented by the purple arrow) are pulling the two yellow points at either end towards each other, pulling the lower linkage up, driving the mechanism out and forwards.

When the arm is stowed, the same spring pulls the driven linkage backwards and up, pulling the arm in and down towards the stowed configuration.

Because this spring acts opposite to gravity, the two effects combine to make the mechanism largely stable in any position. Combined with the force needed to backdrive the gearbox that drives the arm, the intake should stay in position without needing to power the arm motor.

Untie the tubing added earlier and adjust how taut it is until it is just tight enough that they successfully hold the arm up in its fully deployed position while still allowing the motor to overpower them to drive the arm in and out. We found that a length of about 18” to 19” may be enough to constrain the arm.

Tuning is not a one and done activity. As you practice you will continue to tune it to work best with your field elements. You may also need to devise a system for keeping your turnbuckles in place after tuning them while still keeping them easy to adjust in the future. After arriving at a competition and before your first match, try to participate in practice matches to see how the robot needs to be adjusted for the particular field at the event. Some events also have a specific “field calibration” period specifically for adjusting mechanisms like this - ask a referee or FTA at your event for more details.
